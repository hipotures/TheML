- title: Galactic Sightline Context
- group_name: galactic_sightline_context
- family: sky_position
- summary: Use the object's sky location to express whether the line of sight passes through Milky Way-dominated regions or cleaner extragalactic fields.
- strategy: From equatorial coordinates alpha and delta in degrees, first create a unit-sphere representation to remove the alpha wraparound: x = cos(delta_rad) * cos(alpha_rad), y = cos(delta_rad) * sin(alpha_rad), z = sin(delta_rad). Then convert the same position to Galactic coordinates using the standard J2000 equatorial-to-galactic rotation matrix and derive Galactic longitude l and latitude b in degrees. Build the group from: raw b and |b|; sin(l), cos(l), sin(b), cos(b); angular distance to the Galactic plane as |b|; angular distance to the Galactic center and anticenter via great-circle separation from (l,b) = (0,0) and (180,0); and angular distance to the north and south Galactic poles via separation from (0,90) and (0,-90). Add coarse spatial regime indicators by binning |b| into [0,10), [10,20), [20,35), [35,50), [50,70), [70,90] degrees and l into 12 equal 30-degree sectors, plus the crossed regime label of |b|-band with l-sector. Edge handling: convert degrees to radians before trig, keep alpha modulo 360, clip any cosine argument used in arccos to [-1,1], and treat boundary values with left-closed/right-open bins except the final bin which includes 90 degrees.
- expected_signal: Balanced accuracy can benefit because stars are concentrated toward the Galactic plane while galaxies and QSOs are more common in high-latitude sightlines, so this group may especially improve STAR recall without relying on target encoding. The trigonometric and distance-based features also express smooth spatial structure better than raw alpha and delta, which can help separate physically different observing directions.
- risk: These features can partially learn survey footprint and observing-selection artifacts rather than pure astrophysics, so they may overfit if train and test sky coverage differ. They may also be redundant when photometry already strongly identifies classes, limiting incremental gain.
